# Personas and journeys

This note captures the current working position on personas, segments and journeys.

It is intentionally provisional. Do not create canonical persona models or static journey maps until the repository has enough evidence-linked needs, behaviours, pain points, contexts and outcomes to support them.

## Current position

Personas and journeys should be treated as design artefacts or generated views over the evidence-backed knowledge objects in the repository.

They should not be forced into static forms too early.

The repository should first preserve:

- evidence
- user needs
- civic needs
- behaviours
- pain points
- insights
- need patterns
- context
- outcomes
- value blockers

Personas and journeys can then be created from those objects when they are useful for design, communication, synthesis or opportunity framing.

## Personas and segmentation

Personas are context-specific artefacts used for design.

They should not be treated as fictional summaries or fixed audience types unless the modelling approach is clear and evidence-backed.

For this repository, useful personas are likely to emerge from segmentation of:

- user needs
- behaviours
- capability differences
- decision agency
- support networks
- parent/carer role
- communication needs
- advocacy capacity
- confidence and trust
- previous system experience
- service or policy constraints
- context-specific outcome risks

This means personas may be better understood as outcome-relevant segments or contextual archetypes, not static fictional profiles.

## Example segmentation dimensions

For SEND pathway planning, useful dimensions may include:

| Dimension | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Young person's decision agency | Whether the young person is choosing, co-choosing, resisting, deferring or having decisions made for them |
| Parent/carer role | Whether parents are enabling, translating, advocating, deciding, overburdened, excluded or absent |
| Communication needs | Whether standard planning formats allow the young person to express preferences |
| Capability profile | What level of abstraction, comparison or future planning is realistic |
| Support network | Who helps interpret options, rights and consequences |
| Previous system experience | Whether the family has trust, fear, fatigue, learned helplessness or adversarial behaviour |
| Route constraints | Whether options are genuinely available or only nominally available |
| Confidence and aspiration | Whether the issue is information, belief, trauma, expectation, fear or practical feasibility |

These dimensions should be observed through research. Do not turn them into a fixed segmentation model too early.

## Journeys as generated views

High-level experience journeys should be built from the ground up from evidence-backed objects.

A journey view may draw on:

- journey stages currently observed in the research
- user needs at each stage
- pain points and blockers
- behaviours and coping strategies
- civic needs and institutional obligations
- decision-shaped needs
- context and constraints
- outcomes and value blockers
- opportunities and solution responses

Journey phases are working frames, not permanent truth.

As new evidence, user needs and behaviours are added, the journey structure may need to change.

## Why static journey maps become stale

A static board or one-off journey map can be useful for workshop alignment, but it may become out of date quickly if:

- new evidence changes the stages
- new user needs alter the shape of the experience
- a pain point moves earlier or later in the journey
- different segments follow different routes
- policy or service constraints change
- generated views need to show different actor or capability perspectives

The repository should therefore preserve the underlying objects and relationships, not only the drawn map.

## Operational rule

Use journeys as views over the repository, not as the primary source of truth.

Use personas as context-specific design artefacts derived from segmentation, not as canonical fictional users.

When a journey or persona view changes because the underlying evidence or need structure changes, record the change in the view or PR summary. Do not silently rewrite stable research objects to fit the view.

## Relationship to opportunities and solutions

Journey views can help identify opportunity areas and solution responses.

However, opportunities and solutions should trace back to the underlying needs, pain points, behaviours, civic needs and evidence, not only to a journey-stage label.

For example:

```text
Journey stage: Compare options
Need: Need to judge which options are realistic
Pain point: Options are listed without suitability or support implications
Behaviour: Parent relies on informal networks to interpret options
Opportunity: Help families compare routes by suitability, support and consequences
```

The stage helps organise the view. The evidence-backed objects carry the reasoning.

## LLM handling

When an LLM creates or updates a journey or persona view, it should:

- state that the artefact is a generated or synthesised view
- link back to the evidence-backed objects it uses
- preserve uncertainty and review state
- avoid inventing personas without evidence
- avoid making journey stages look permanent where the evidence is still emerging
- avoid changing canonical user need wording to fit a journey stage or persona story
