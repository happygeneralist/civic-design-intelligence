# How this system uses AI

Draft public page. Not currently linked from the public homepage.

Civic design intelligence uses AI to help structure, connect and query public-service knowledge.

It does not use AI to replace evidence, validate findings, make decisions automatically or decide what people need.

The purpose is to make civic knowledge more traceable, reviewable and useful, so public institutions can make better decisions about services, policy, operations and public outcomes.

## Summary

Public-service knowledge is often spread across research reports, lived experience, complaints, policy documents, operational data, inspection findings, design histories and staff memory.

AI can help work with that material at a scale and pace that would be difficult manually.

But AI is only useful if the knowledge it works with is structured, governed and traceable.

This system creates that knowledge layer.

It helps turn fragmented material into structured knowledge objects such as:

- user needs
- civic needs
- capability or life-need links
- behaviours
- pain points
- evidence notes
- insights
- value dimensions
- decision evidence
- outcome links

These objects make it easier for people and machines to understand what is known, what is uncertain, what is contested and what needs review.

## What AI helps with

AI can help with practical research and knowledge-management tasks, such as:

- breaking down research into candidate needs, pain points, behaviours and insights
- finding possible relationships between evidence and knowledge objects
- suggesting links between needs, civic needs, capabilities, outcomes and value dimensions
- identifying weak, missing or unsupported evidence
- comparing overlapping objects
- drafting summaries for human review
- generating questions for researchers or reviewers
- helping maintain dashboards and repository views
- querying the repository to support decision-making

AI is useful here because public-service knowledge is complex, dispersed and hard to keep current manually.

The goal is not to make AI authoritative. The goal is to make structured civic knowledge easier to maintain and use.

## What AI must not do

AI must not be treated as evidence.

AI must not:

- validate user needs, civic needs, pain points or insights by itself
- upgrade confidence or evidence strength without human review
- remove caveats or uncertainty from research claims
- rewrite lived-experience evidence in ways that change meaning
- make service, policy or operational decisions automatically
- decide what public institutions should prioritise
- turn weak assumptions into validated knowledge
- hide when it has materially shaped a change

The repository follows a simple principle:

> AI can help create and connect candidate knowledge. People remain responsible for interpretation, validation and decisions.

## Why structured knowledge matters

AI works poorly when public-service knowledge is only held in disconnected documents, slide decks, chat transcripts or tacit institutional memory.

Without structure, AI can easily:

- overclaim weak evidence
- flatten important distinctions
- confuse user needs with civic needs
- treat pain points as isolated anecdotes
- miss capability or rights implications
- ignore uncertainty
- optimise for what is easy to measure rather than what matters

Civic design intelligence reduces these risks by making knowledge objects explicit, linkable and reviewable.

Each object can carry metadata about evidence, status, maturity, lifecycle state, related needs, related pain points, related outcomes and review history.

This makes the repository more useful to people and more legible to machines.

## Civic AI and this repository

Civic AI is a broad term. It can refer to AI systems that support public participation, collective intelligence, community action, public-service decision-making or civic transformation.

This repository is not a civic AI product by itself.

It is the operational knowledge layer that civic AI needs if it is going to be grounded in evidence and public value.

Civic AI can only be as good as the civic knowledge environment it works within. If the knowledge base is fragmented, outdated, ambiguous or untraceable, AI may accelerate confusion rather than improve decisions.

Civic design intelligence helps by creating structured, reviewable civic knowledge that AI tools can use more responsibly.

## A layer for computation

For knowledge to be computable, it needs more than prose.

It needs stable objects and relationships that can be queried, compared and reasoned over.

For example, a user need can be linked to:

- the evidence that supports it
- the civic need it relates to
- the capability or life need it protects
- the pain points that block it
- the service areas affected
- the outcomes it contributes to
- the value dimensions it supports
- the decisions that have used it
- the assumptions or uncertainties attached to it

This does not mean reducing human needs to simplistic scores.

It means preserving enough structure for people and machines to ask better questions.

Examples include:

- Which civic needs are currently unsupported?
- Which outcomes depend on this need being met?
- Which pain points block the greatest amount of public value?
- Which service decisions rely on weak evidence?
- Which needs are being repeatedly erased as work moves from research into policy or delivery?
- Which capabilities are weakened if this pain point remains unresolved?

This is why the repository treats needs, pain points and evidence as first-class objects rather than disposable research notes.

## A layer for simulation

Simulation can be useful in public-service contexts, but only if it represents the right things.

A simulation may show that a process is faster, cheaper or more efficient while missing that it has increased burden, reduced agency, weakened redress, ignored a civic obligation or made a life outcome worse.

For simulation to support public value, it needs structured knowledge about:

- user needs
- civic needs
- capabilities
- rights and responsibilities
- service constraints
- pain points
- burden
- risk
- value dimensions
- intended outcomes
- unintended consequences

The repository can become part of the preparation layer for simulation by making these relationships explicit.

A simulation should not only ask:

> What happens to throughput, cost or demand?

It should also ask:

> What happens to needs, capabilities, burden, fairness, agency and outcomes?

Civic design intelligence helps make those questions computable without pretending they are simple.

## Needs erasure and AI

One of the risks in public services is needs erasure.

Needs erasure happens when a person's real need, civic entitlement, capability requirement or life condition is lost, flattened or made invisible as work moves through institutional processes.

AI can make this worse if it is used on poorly structured material.

For example, AI might summarise evidence in a way that removes uncertainty, collapse lived experience into a process category, or optimise around the service's view of the problem rather than the person's life context.

The repository helps reduce this risk by preserving links between:

- evidence
- user needs
- civic needs
- capability or life needs
- pain points
- value dimensions
- decisions
- outcomes

This supports the Lives to be Lived framing: public services should be judged by whether they help people live lives with dignity, agency, safety, belonging and realistic possibility, not only by whether institutional processes complete.

## Human responsibility

People remain responsible for interpretation, validation and decisions.

The repository can support AI-assisted work, but it should preserve a clear separation between:

- evidence
- AI-assisted synthesis
- human analysis
- reviewed knowledge
- validated findings
- decisions

This matters because public-service decisions affect people's lives, rights, access, dignity and opportunities.

AI can support judgement. It should not replace accountability.

## Public and partner contribution

In the future, this system could support public and partner contributions.

People, families, professionals, advocates and third-sector organisations may be able to submit corrections, evidence, challenges or lived-experience signals.

AI may help triage, cluster or route those contributions.

But contribution does not automatically become truth. Public and partner insight should be captured, linked and reviewed before it changes the canonical knowledge layer.

This makes collective intelligence more usable while preserving safety, privacy and accountability.

## Responsible use principles

The system should use AI in ways that are:

- evidence-linked
- reviewable
- conservative about validation
- transparent about uncertainty
- safe for sensitive public-service contexts
- careful with lived experience
- accountable to human review
- open to challenge and correction
- grounded in public value

The aim is not to use AI because it is novel.

The aim is to use AI where it helps public institutions preserve, connect and act on civic knowledge more responsibly.

## What this means for the MVP

The current MVP does not need to build a full AI product, simulation engine or public contribution platform.

The immediate work is more practical:

- keep evidence separate from interpretation
- create stable knowledge objects
- link needs to civic needs, capabilities, pain points and outcomes where useful
- use conservative review and maturity states
- avoid treating AI output as evidence
- record material semantic changes
- keep unclear material in the inbox until it can be reviewed

This creates the foundation for safer AI-assisted analysis later.

## The public value of this approach

Used carefully, AI can help public institutions work with complexity without losing accountability.

Civic design intelligence helps by giving AI something better to work with: structured, traceable and contestable knowledge about needs, pain, evidence, value and outcomes.

That makes it possible to ask better questions, challenge weaker decisions and understand where public services are failing to support the lives people are trying to live.

The goal is not automated government.

The goal is better civic reasoning.
