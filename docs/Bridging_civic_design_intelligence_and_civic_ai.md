# Bridging civic design intelligence and civic AI

This document explains how the civic design intelligence system relates to civic AI, collective intelligence, open government and future-facing civic systems work.

It is not intended to reposition the repository as an AI product. It clarifies how the operational knowledge layer in this repository connects to broader ambitions around civic transformation, public participation, institutional learning and responsible use of AI.

## Core position

Civic AI and civic futures work often imagines new forms of collective action and institutional transformation.

Civic design intelligence works one layer down: it creates the structured, traceable civic knowledge infrastructure that public institutions need in order to understand needs, act on evidence, learn over time and use AI responsibly.

The repository is therefore not a competing future-facing concept. It is a practical dependency layer for making those futures actionable, governable and accountable.

## The missing operational layer

Many public-service transformation efforts move quickly from evidence or lived experience into strategy, policy, service design, AI tools or innovation programmes.

The weak point is often the layer between evidence and action.

Public institutions need a durable way to know:

- what people need
- what civic responsibilities or public outcomes are implicated
- where pain, burden, harm or exclusion occurs
- what evidence supports or challenges the current understanding
- what is assumed, uncertain, contested, reviewed or validated
- where public-service value is blocked
- which decisions have relied on which knowledge
- when understanding has changed

Without this layer, future-facing civic work risks being disconnected from operational reality.

Civic design intelligence provides this layer by treating needs, pain points, evidence, behaviours, insights, value gaps and civic outcomes as traceable knowledge objects rather than disposable research artefacts.

## Relationship to civic AI

Civic AI can help analyse, synthesise, coordinate, simulate or support civic action. However, AI systems are only as useful and accountable as the knowledge environment they operate within.

If public-service knowledge is fragmented, outdated, ambiguous or untraceable, AI may accelerate institutional confusion rather than improve civic decision-making.

Civic design intelligence supports responsible civic AI by creating conditions where AI can operate over knowledge that is:

- evidence-linked
- status-labelled
- reviewable
- contestable
- semantically stable
- open to supersession
- connected to decisions and outcomes

This matters because AI should not be treated as a substitute for public-service understanding. It should help institutions work with structured, accountable knowledge.

## AI-readiness risks and repository responses

| Civic AI risk | Civic design intelligence response |
| --- | --- |
| AI overclaims weak or immature material | Use evidence maturity, review status and conservative validation states |
| AI flattens meaning or rewrites needs | Treat user needs, civic needs and pain points as persistent objects with semantic change logs where needed |
| AI amplifies outdated assumptions | Support lifecycle states, supersession and links to current evidence |
| AI obscures accountability | Use Git history, object change logs and LLM intervention logs for material semantic changes |
| AI cannot distinguish user needs from civic needs | Maintain an explicit distinction between user needs and civic needs |
| AI treats pain as anecdotal noise | Treat pain points as condition and risk objects that may be active, unresolved, resolved, dormant or recurring |
| AI optimises for local service efficiency while missing public value | Link needs and pain points to value dimensions, civic outcomes and decision evidence |

## Relationship to collective intelligence

Collective intelligence is not only about gathering more input. Public, professional and partner knowledge needs a route into institutional understanding.

Civic design intelligence provides a way to metabolise contributions without allowing them to directly overwrite reviewed knowledge.

A future contribution model should distinguish between:

| Layer | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Public and partner contribution | People and organisations submit lived experience, corrections, evidence, challenges and signals |
| Triage | Contributions are checked for relevance, safety, privacy and evidential value |
| Stewardship | Researchers, service teams or accountable owners decide how contributions affect knowledge objects |
| Traceability | Changes are linked to evidence, rationale and review status |
| Publication | The repository shows what is known, uncertain, contested, outdated or unresolved |

This makes collective input institutionally usable while preserving accountability.

## Relationship to open government

The repository can support open government by making public-service reasoning more visible and contestable.

Over time, it should help public institutions show:

- what they believe people need
- what evidence supports that understanding
- where evidence is weak, missing or contested
- what pain points remain unresolved
- where services are failing to deliver public value
- what decisions have been made using which knowledge
- where external contributors have challenged or improved the evidence base
- how institutional understanding has changed

This is not transparency as publication alone. It is transparency as inspectable public reasoning.

## Relationship to institutional transformation

Future-facing civic systems work often focuses on the structures that shape public life, including policy, governance, finance, regulation, commissioning, behaviours and institutional logic.

Civic design intelligence connects to this by showing how evidence about needs and pain implicates institutional levers.

Examples:

| Knowledge object | Possible institutional implication |
| --- | --- |
| Repeated parent or carer pain point | May indicate an operational failure, policy ambiguity, capacity gap or communication failure |
| Unmet civic need | May indicate a public-outcome risk, statutory delivery issue or accountability gap |
| Weak evidence area | May indicate a need for further research, data-sharing, frontline engagement or public contribution |
| Value gap | May indicate a service design, access, eligibility, inclusion or communications failure |
| Recurring unresolved pain | May indicate a structural constraint rather than an isolated delivery issue |

The repository does not need to solve every institutional lever during the MVP. It should preserve enough structure to make those relationships visible later.

## Civic learning loop

The system should be understood as a civic learning infrastructure, not only as a repository.

A civic learning loop works like this:

1. Evidence comes in from research, lived experience, service data, frontline professionals, third-sector organisations, public reports, inspections, complaints and design histories.
2. Evidence is triaged and linked to structured knowledge objects.
3. Knowledge objects inform priorities, service decisions, policy questions, operational changes, design histories or further research.
4. Decisions and interventions create new evidence about what changed and what remains unresolved.
5. The knowledge base is updated, challenged, refined or superseded.

This loop connects operational evidence work to adaptive governance and institutional learning.

## Distinguishing evidence, knowledge, intelligence and action

The system should avoid treating all material as intelligence.

| Term | Meaning in this system |
| --- | --- |
| Evidence | Source material such as research, data, testimony, observation, complaints, reports or service records |
| Knowledge object | Structured interpretation of evidence, such as a need, pain point, behaviour, insight or value gap |
| Intelligence | A connected view that supports judgement, prioritisation or action |
| Decision | A choice made using that intelligence |
| Outcome | The intended or observed public effect of acting on the decision |
| Learning | What changes in the knowledge base after new evidence or outcome information is reviewed |

Civic design intelligence does not treat intelligence as raw data or AI output. It treats intelligence as structured, traceable, action-oriented understanding about civic needs, service conditions and public value.

## Rosetta stone: connecting future-facing language to repository practice

| Future-facing civic systems language | Repository practice |
| --- | --- |
| Collective intelligence | Public, professional and partner contributions metabolised into traceable knowledge |
| Civic AI | AI operating over structured, reviewed and evidence-linked civic knowledge |
| Institutional transformation | Better decisions through persistent knowledge of needs, pain, value and outcomes |
| Adaptive governance | Knowledge objects that evolve as evidence changes |
| Open government | Publicly inspectable reasoning, uncertainty, contestability and change |
| Systems change | Linking pain, needs and value gaps to institutional levers |
| Public value | Mapping whether services actually meet needs in useful, accessible, ethical and outcome-supporting ways |

## What this means for the MVP

The MVP should not try to build the full civic AI, contribution or institutional transformation model.

The immediate priority remains practical and concrete:

- preserve evidence separately from interpretation
- create stable first-class objects for user needs, civic needs and pain points
- use conservative maturity and review states
- record material meaning changes through object change logs
- use LLM intervention logs only when LLMs make or propose material semantic changes
- link needs, pain points and evidence to value where useful
- leave immature material in the inbox rather than forcing premature structure

The broader bridge should live in the vision, roadmap and documentation. The repository structure should remain lightweight until real use shows which additional objects or relationships are needed.

## Summary

Civic design intelligence is not another civic AI concept.

It is the operational knowledge layer that public institutions need before AI, policy, service design, innovation or civic transformation work can be trusted to improve outcomes.

Its distinctive contribution is to make civic knowledge persistent, traceable, contestable and usable for decisions.

That makes the work concrete and mundane by design. The purpose is not to perform civic imagination. The purpose is to make better civic futures institutionally possible.
