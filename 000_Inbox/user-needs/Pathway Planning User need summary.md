This is a consolidated list of **pathway planning user needs** pulled from [Pathway Planning guidance](https://gatesheadcs.trixonline.co.uk/chapter/pathway-plan-guidance) and primary user research studies found in [001_Research_studies]. 

> These have been summarised with the help of [Gov User Needs LLM](https://chatgpt.com/g/g-673f960b95b48191802b8036a6bdc3a8-gov-user-needs) and should be **handled as assumptions until fully anchored to the research**.

## A. Cross-cutting / high-level needs

- **Young people need their route into adulthood to lead to a life they can manage safely and as independently as possible** (housing, money, learning, work, relationships).
- **Young people need plans to be shaped with them, not just for them**, so the goals and actions reflect what they actually want to work towards
- **All professionals involved (including teachers) need clarity about the part they play in a young person’s pathway**, so key actions are actually carried out and not duplicated or missed.
- **Young people and families need planning to recognise both practical and emotional aspects**, so progress can continue even when the future feels frightening, overwhelming or uncertain.
- **Young people and families need planning to anticipate possible changes and disruptions** (moves, exclusions, health changes, custody, family changes), so they don’t lose progress every time circumstances shift.
- **Services need to recognise that young people’s needs and circumstances vary widely**, so planning feels relevant and not like a generic one-size-fits-all process.
## B. Young people – creating the pathway / plan

- **Young people need to influence the goals and actions in their plan**, so it reflects what they actually care about, not only what adults think should happen.
- **Young people need to describe what helps and what makes things harder in learning and daily life**, so support around them can be shaped to work for them.
- **Young people need planning processes they can cope with emotionally and practically**, so they can take part even if formal meetings, big groups or long discussions are difficult.
- **Young people need personal and sensitive information to be handled in a way that protects their dignity and privacy**, while still allowing adults to support them.
- **Young people need to explore possible futures starting from their interests and strengths**, so they can see realistic pathways that feel meaningful rather than only being offered generic options.
- **Young people need pathways to include less typical or creative ambitions as well as familiar options**, so they don’t feel their real interests are being dismissed as unrealistic.
- **Young people need help working out which interests could become work or study options and which are better kept as hobbies**, so they can make informed choices about their direction.
- **Young people need to see that there is more than one possible route into adult life**, so they don’t feel everything depends on a single, fragile plan.
- **Young people need to know there are alternative or backup options linked to what matters to them**, so a setback in one route doesn’t make them give up on their overall goals.
- **Young people need planning to take friendships, relationships and belonging into account**, so they don’t feel forced to choose between progress and staying connected to people who matter to them.
- **Young people need opportunities to think about the future in manageable steps**, so they can move forward even when long-term planning feels frightening or overwhelming.
- **Young people need ways to access pathway information and support directly, not only through parents or carers**, so their progress isn’t limited by others’ time, capacity or willingness.

## C. Young people – using the pathway / communicating it (especially with school/college)

- **Young people need to see clearly what has been agreed for their education, training, work and support**, so they can decide whether to take up opportunities, question arrangements that don’t feel right, and plan their own next steps.
- **Young people need to convey their key needs and arrangements to new teachers or settings without repeatedly retelling distressing or highly personal information.**
- **Young people need regular chances to say whether actions in the plan are helping or not**, so changes can be made before they disengage or reach crisis.
- **Young people need information about options and next steps that they can use even when reading or concentration is difficult**, so their difficulties don’t prevent them from taking part in planning.
- **Young people need to feel confident that the information they act on (about courses, benefits, jobs, etc.) is reliable and safe**, so they are not put at risk by misleading or outdated advice.
- **Young people need to be able to find possibilities that fit them even if they don’t know the right terms or spellings**, so limited vocabulary or literacy doesn’t block them from discovering opportunities.
## D. Parents / carers – creating the pathway / plan

- **Parents and carers need to contribute what they know about their child’s strengths, difficulties, routines and aspirations**, so plans build on what already works rather than starting from scratch every time.
- **Parents and carers need plans that take account of the wider family situation** (transport, work, finances, siblings, caring responsibilities), so agreed actions are realistic and sustainable.
- **Parents and carers need upcoming transitions (e.g. new school, post-16 move, leaving care) to be anticipated in the plan**, so support doesn’t have to be rebuilt at the last minute.
- **Parents and carers need to see the main steps in their child’s journey over the coming years and roughly when they are likely to happen**, so they can prepare evidence, forms and conversations in good time instead of reacting in crisis.
- **Parents and carers need to know that alternative routes exist if a particular course, application or assessment doesn’t work out**, so they don’t feel their child’s future depends on a single outcome.
- **Parents and carers need to know where to go first when they have a question about their child’s pathway**, so they aren’t passed repeatedly between services in search of basic answers.
- **Parents and carers need information they can use even when they are tired, stressed or overwhelmed**, so they can still take practical steps for their child.
- **Parents and carers need information that is clearly addressed to them in their role**, alongside information aimed at the young person, so each can see what is expected of them.
- **Parents and carers need to be able to get personalised help with complex decisions when general information is not enough**, so they are not left trying to interpret systems entirely on their own.
- **Parents and carers need planning around benefits, funding and legal changes to be predictable and transparent**, so official letters and assessments don’t arrive as constant shocks.
- **Parents and carers need support to think beyond immediate crises**, so their child’s long-term future remains in view even when daily life is difficult.

## E. Parents / carers – using the pathway / communicating it

- **Parents and carers need to see clearly which actions different organisations (school, college, local authority, health services) have agreed to take**, so they know who to approach when something is not happening.
- **Parents and carers need a straightforward way to raise concerns or disagreements about how the plan is being put into practice**, so problems can be addressed early rather than escalating into conflict.
- **Parents and carers need to know when reviews and key decision points will happen**, so they can gather evidence, prepare questions and involve their child appropriately.

## F. Practitioners / social workers / personal advisers – creating the pathway / plan

- **Practitioners need to bring together information about a young person’s needs, strengths and circumstances from education, health, social care, housing and finance**, so the young person receives a joined-up plan rather than conflicting or piecemeal support.
- **Practitioners need to agree realistic outcomes for education, training and employment with both the young person and education providers**, so the plan sets goals that can actually be worked towards.
- **Practitioners need to distinguish between urgent actions and longer-term work in the plan**, so effort is focused where it will make the most difference.
- **Practitioners need to record who is responsible for each action and the timescale for it**, so progress can be monitored and followed up without relying on memory.
- **Practitioners need planning processes that can capture and hold disagreements between the young person and professionals**, so concerns are visible and can be revisited rather than ignored.
- **Practitioners need plans that can be updated as the young person’s situation, wishes or risks change**, so support stays relevant over time.
- **Practitioners need to involve key people in the young person’s life (family, carers, school staff, health practitioners, advocates) in ways that respect the young person’s wishes**, so important perspectives are included without overwhelming them.

## G. Practitioners / social workers / personal advisers – using & communicating the plan

- **Practitioners need to share with schools only the parts of a plan that they need to act on**, so teachers can support the young person while the young person’s privacy is protected.
- **Practitioners need to know whether actions that depend on schools or colleges are actually happening**, so they can step in early if the young person’s placement or engagement is at risk.
- **Practitioners need to review plans when significant events occur** (such as a move of placement, exclusion, course change, or entry to or release from custody), so the pathway stays realistic and safe.
- **Practitioners need a clear record of why changes were made to a plan**, so later workers and agencies can understand decisions and avoid repeating past mistakes.
- **For looked-after and care-experienced young people, practitioners need pathway plans that cover accommodation, income, support and education or training together over the relevant age range**, so their route into adulthood does not break when services change.
- **Practitioners need some way to keep a route back open for young people who disengage or lose contact**, so those young people can return for support when they are ready.

## H. Teachers / school & college staff – creating / contributing to the pathway

- **Teachers and other education staff need to know which parts of a young person’s plan require changes in teaching, curriculum, timetable or support**, so they can adjust how the school or college works with that young person.
- **Education staff need to see how the aims in a pathway plan relate to existing documents** (EHC plans, PEPs, behaviour plans, etc.), so they are not working to conflicting sets of objectives.
- **Education staff need to know what information about the young person’s progress, attendance, behaviour and aspirations they are expected to contribute**, so they can gather it routinely rather than at the last minute.
- **Education staff need planning conversations that connect a young person’s interests and strengths to realistic courses, qualifications and experiences the setting can offer**, so learning feels purposeful to the young person.
- **Education staff need awareness of realistic post-school options for young people with different needs**, so they can help students and families think beyond the immediate next step.

## I. Teachers / school & college staff – using & communicating the plan

- **Education staff need a concise picture of each young person’s key needs, strengths and agreed priorities for education**, so they can factor them into everyday classroom and pastoral practice.
- **Education staff need clarity about what the school or college has agreed to do differently for the young person**, so they can build those actions into planning, teaching and support.
- **Education staff need simple ways to say when aspects of the plan are not workable in practice or are not helping the young person**, so adjustments can be negotiated rather than the plan being quietly ignored.
- **Education staff need to know when reviews or key meetings about the young person’s future are taking place and what their contribution should be**, so they can bring useful information and not miss important decisions.
- **Education staff need enough time within their workload and processes to familiarise themselves with plans and act on them**, so planning leads to actual changes, not just paperwork.

## J. System / service-level needs

- **Services need consistent ways for plans to be recorded and shared across organisations**, so professionals can access up-to-date information without relying on ad-hoc personal systems.
- **Services need to identify points at which a young person’s situation or data suggests rising risk** (e.g. falling attendance, exclusions, unstable placements), so plans can be reviewed before crises occur.
- **Services need to align pathway planning with existing statutory and institutional processes** (EHC reviews, PEPs, annual reviews, etc.), so young people and families are not repeatedly asked the same questions by different professionals.
- **Services need to support young people with very different profiles of need in ways that still feel relevant to each of them**, so families are not swamped by information that does not apply to their situation.
- **Services need confidence that the guidance and information they give to families and young people is accurate, up-to-date and consistent across agencies**, so people are not given contradictory messages.
- **Services need to ensure that information about local and regional education, training, employment and support opportunities is visible to the professionals, young people and families who need it**, so young people are not limited simply because options are hidden or poorly described.
- **Services need to make it possible for young people to resume education or training later on and reconnect with planning after breaks or drop-outs**, so a setback does not permanently close down their future options.
