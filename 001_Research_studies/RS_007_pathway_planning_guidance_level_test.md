## Method

The experiment used a competing propositions test. Participants were shown three low-fidelity value propositions representing different levels and types of pathway-planning support.

The source describes the method as short, moderated interviews using realistic pathway planning scenarios. Sessions were remote and approximately 30 minutes.

## Learning question

The core learning question was:

> What level of support do young people and families need to understand their options and feel confident about their next step?

## Assumption and hypothesis

The experiment was anchored on this assumption:

> Some planning needs can be met through clearer guidance. Other needs may still require one-to-one support.

The hypothesis was:

> Clear or personalised guidance will help many young people and families understand their options and next steps before they need one-to-one support.

The source states that this hypothesis was about sufficiency and sequencing of support, not removing or replacing relational services.

## Propositions tested

The experiment tested three value propositions:

1. Step-by-step pathway guide
2. Personalised pathway helper
3. Pathway planning conversation

The source states that these were abstract, low-fidelity propositions. The experiment was not testing finished content, interfaces or a working service.

## Intended evidence

The experiment was designed to gather qualitative evidence about:

- which propositions participants understood and valued
- whether participants felt clearer or more confident
- whether the described support would be enough to get started
- when participants would still need to speak to someone
- whether the support would help participants ask better questions or take a next step

## Success criteria

The source states that the experiment would be considered successful if the team could clearly describe:

- which planning needs may be met through structured guidance
- which may benefit from personalisation
- which still require one-to-one support

Success was not defined as selecting a single winning proposition.
